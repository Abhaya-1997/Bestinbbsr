🔍 How to Spot a Trustworthy Mechanic

Before you hand over your bike to anyone on this route, run through this checklist:

  • 🔩
    He shows you the old part A good mechanic removes the worn-out component and places it in front of you before installing the new one. This is non-negotiable. If they dispose of the old part without showing you, that's a red flag.
  • 💬
    He gives a quote before starting Not a surprise bill at the end — a rough estimate upfront. Any mechanic who says "we'll see when we open it up" without giving you a ceiling number is not playing fair.
  • 📋
    He keeps a service register The best mechanics note the date, job done, and parts used for every vehicle they work on. Ask to see it. If it exists, you're in the right place.
  • 🚫
    He doesn't push 5 things at once If you came for a chain adjustment and suddenly need "6 other urgent repairs," be careful. Honest mechanics fix what you brought the bike for, then mention other issues separately without pressure.
  • 👥
    His workshop has regulars A crowded shop with familiar faces is the best review you can get. Ask nearby shopkeepers or auto drivers who they personally use — that's community-verified trust.

⚠️ Any mechanic who refuses to give a quote before starting work is a red flag

  1. Ask upfront, always. "What will this cost me roughly?" is a completely fair question before you hand over your bike. Any mechanic worth his time will give you a number — or at least a range.
  2. Itemised bills are your right. If the final bill seems high, ask them to write out what was done and what each item cost. This alone tends to reduce inflated quotes.
  3. Original vs. duplicate parts. Always ask explicitly. Original parts cost more but last significantly longer. A mechanic who tells you the difference honestly is one you can trust.
  4. Cross-check tyre prices. Tyre costs are the most commonly inflated line item at roadside workshops. Know what a tube and tyre costs at a proper shop before you need it on the road.
